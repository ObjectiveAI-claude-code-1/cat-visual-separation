Examine whether the cat's fur differs sufficiently in color, brightness, and tone from the surfaces and spaces directly behind and beneath it so that the cat's silhouette and edges are legible to the viewer at a glance. A white cat on a dark bedspread has strong contrast; a gray cat on a gray couch has almost none; a ginger cat on warm-toned hardwood sits in a dangerous middle ground. Score this dimension highly when the boundary where the cat ends and the background begins is immediately readable, and score it low when the cat's form dissolves into similarly colored or similarly lit surroundings and the viewer must strain to trace the animal's outline.

2. Evaluate the pattern and texture distinction between the cat's coat and the surfaces in its environment. Determine whether the visual character of the cat's fur — its markings, its texture, the scale and rhythm of its patterns — is distinct from the textures around it, independent of overall color similarity. A tabby on a mottled rug, a calico on a patchwork quilt, or a long-haired cat on a shaggy blanket can lose separation even when their colors differ because the visual frequency of the patterns rhymes too closely. Score this dimension highly when the cat's surface reads as visually distinct from its surroundings in pattern and texture, and score it low when the cat's coat and its environment share a similar visual rhythm that causes the cat to become embedded in the scene's texture rather than sitting atop it as a distinct subject.

3. Evaluate the scene clarity and presence of competing visual elements in the frame. Determine whether the cat commands the top of the scene's visual hierarchy or whether other elements — other animals, people, bright objects, glowing screens, bold graphic patterns, or vivid colors elsewhere in the frame — rival the cat for the viewer's attention. This is not a demand for minimalism; a cat on a sunlit windowsill with a garden behind it can still be the clear subject if nothing in that garden matches the cat's visual weight. Score this dimension highly when the cat is the undeniable focal point of the image with no competing elements that hijack the viewer's gaze, and score it low when the scene is crowded with distractions that demote the cat from subject to bystander.
